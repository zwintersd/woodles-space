The Challenger Disaster

✦

A Cold Morning, a Reversed Recommendation, Seventy-Three Seconds

✦

Seventy-three seconds after liftoff, on a morning colder than any shuttle had ever launched into, a rubber ring smaller than a dinner plate let go, and a mission carrying a schoolteacher into orbit ended in the sky over the Atlantic. This isn't really a story about a part that failed. It's a story about what a written, dated, specific warning has to survive on its way from an engineer's desk to a launch director's word — and about how close that particular warning came to being enough.

1

What Happened, and Where the Name Came From

On January 28, 1986 — 39 years ago this past winter — Space Shuttle Challenger lifted off from Kennedy Space Center at 11:38 a.m. Eastern time on mission STS-51-L, carrying seven crew: commander Francis "Dick" Scobee, pilot Michael J. Smith, mission specialists Judith Resnik, Ellison Onizuka, and Ronald McNair, payload specialist Gregory Jarvis, and Sharon Christa McAuliffe, a social studies teacher flying as the first participant in NASA's Teacher in Space Project. At T+73 seconds, roughly 46,000 ft (14 km) up, a seal in the right solid rocket booster's aft joint failed. Hot gas breached the joint, the external fuel tank ruptured, and the vehicle broke apart in flight.

The orbiter itself was the third vehicle to carry the name, each one built to go somewhere no one had mapped yet:

1858 HMS Challenger — Royal Navy corvette; its 1872–76 voyage founded modern oceanography→ 1972 Apollo 17 Lunar Module Challenger — the last crewed lunar landing→ 1983 Space Shuttle Orbiter Challenger
2

Where the Plan and Reality Split

The joint that failed wasn't experimental. It had flown on every shuttle mission before this one. What changed was the temperature.

The failure here wasn't a lack of warning. Thiokol's own engineers had put the concern in writing, with a temperature threshold attached, months before launch. The failure was in what a written warning has to survive to still be a warning by the time it reaches a room with the authority to stop a countdown.
3

The Teleconference, and the Morning After

The night before launch, engineers at Morton Thiokol — the SRB's manufacturer, in Utah — held a teleconference with NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center to discuss whether to fly the next morning at all.

The reversal came after Thiokol's senior vice president asked the room for a five-minute, off-the-record caucus that ran closer to thirty — and, by more than one engineer's later account, told the VP of Engineering to take off his engineering hat and put on his management hat. The recommendation that reached NASA that night no longer had a temperature limit attached to it.

4

What It Established, Who It Was For

The sociologist Diane Vaughan later gave the pattern behind the reversal a name: normalization of deviance — the way a system can absorb a documented risk, again and again, without anyone deciding to. Every flight that came back with scorched-but-intact O-rings and no explosion had functioned, whether anyone intended it to or not, as a rewarded trial. String enough unpunished near-misses together, and "acceptable risk" quietly redraws itself around whatever didn't kill anyone yet — not because anyone in the room was careless, but because that's what a long enough run of near-misses does to a group's sense of the odds.

5

The Number Feynman Couldn't Let Go Of

Physicist Richard Feynman served on the Rogers Commission that investigated the disaster, and became fixated on a gap nobody else on the panel wanted to dwell on: how differently NASA's own people rated the odds of the thing he'd just watched happen.

The historical rate — this disaster, and Columbia's in 2003 — landed far closer to the engineers' number than to management's. Feynman's own account of that gap, and of the hearing where he dipped a clamped piece of O-ring material into a glass of ice water on live television to show a room full of officials that the rubber simply stopped springing back near 32°F (0°C), became the most-remembered testimony of the whole investigation.
